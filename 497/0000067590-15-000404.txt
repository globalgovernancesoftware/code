Securities Risk<FONT
style="font-weight:Normal;">. Asset-backed and mortgage-backed securities are fixed income securities that represent an interest in an underlying pool of assets, such as credit card receivables or, in the case of

connection with investments in loans to &#8220;subprime&#8221; borrowers. Certain asset-backed and mortgage-backed securities are subject to the risk that those obligations will be repaid sooner than expected or later
than expected, either of which may result in lower than expected returns. Mortgage-backed securities, because they are backed by mortgage loans, are also subject to risks related to real estate, and securities backed
by private-issued mortgages may experience higher rates of default on the underlying mortgages than securities backed by government-issued mortgages.</FONT></DIV>
<DIV

which, in certain instances, could adversely affect the Portfolio, including its risk profile, expenses and performance. For example, the asset flows may adversely affect performance by requiring the Portfolio to
purchase or sell securities at inopportune times, by otherwise limiting the subadviser&#8217;s ability to fully implement the Portfolio&#8217;s investment strategies, or by requiring the Portfolio to hold a larger
portion of its assets in highly liquid securities than it otherwise would hold. The asset flows may also result in relatively low asset levels and relatively high operating expense ratios for the Portfolio. The
efficient operation of the asset flows depends on active and liquid markets, and if market liquidity is strained the assets flows may not operate as intended which in turn could adversely affect performance.</FONT></DIV>

traditional equity and debt securities.</FONT></DIV>

style="font-weight:Normal;">. A derivative is a financial contract, the value of which depends upon, or is derived from, the value of an underlying asset, reference rate, or index. The use of derivatives involves a
variety of risks, including the risk that: the party on the other side of a derivative transaction will be unable to honor its financial obligation; leverage created by investing in derivatives may result in losses to
the Portfolio; derivatives may be difficult or impossible for the Portfolio to buy or sell at an opportune time or price, and may be difficult to terminate or otherwise offset; derivatives used for hedging may reduce
or magnify losses but also may reduce or eliminate gains; and the price of commodity-linked derivatives may be more volatile than the prices of traditional equity and debt securities.</FONT></DIV>
